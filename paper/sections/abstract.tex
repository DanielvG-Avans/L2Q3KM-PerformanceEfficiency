\begin{abstract}
This study investigates the influence of server-side rendering (SSR) and client-side rendering (CSR) on mobile client-side energy use within a comparable Next.js prototype. Although these render strategies are frequently compared using speed and user-experience metrics, limited attention is given to direct energy-related measurement in a controlled mobile context.

To address this gap, one storefront application was implemented in two functionally equivalent variants: an SSR route and a CSR route sharing the same interface, data model, and scenario structure. The final benchmark was executed on a Samsung Galaxy A53 under battery-powered wireless conditions, with 60 randomized runs across three dataset scenarios and two rendering conditions. Energy was estimated by integrating battery current and voltage over time, while browser-facing metrics such as LCP, FCP, TTFB, and measured navigation response size were collected through the Chrome DevTools Protocol.

The corrected final dataset shows that SSR produced lower median total energy in all three scenarios, with the strongest support in the static and massive workloads, while CSR often retained smaller measured navigation responses and, in the dynamic scenario, lower paint-related timings. The main contribution of this report is therefore not a universal winner, but a more precise description of the trade-off between server-shifted and client-shifted work when mobile energy efficiency is treated as a primary concern.

\end{abstract}
